Document Discovery Journey

A plain-English view of how the GitHub DMS helps employees find the right compliance document, confirm it is current, discover related materials, and know what to do next.

{% include site-nav.html %}
Viewer Problem

People should not need tribal knowledge to find related documents.

Today, a policy search can turn into a scavenger hunt across tools, folders, PDFs, owners, and historical versions. The target DMS should make the current document, related documents, accountable owner, status, and change path visible from one staff-facing portal.

Discovery Path

The user journey starts with a topic, not a repository.

01 Search by topic

Employee searches using business language such as sanctions, complaints, privacy, onboarding, or record retention.

02 Confirm current source

The portal shows the authoritative published version, lifecycle state, owner, effective date, and next review date.

03 See related documents

Metadata connects policies, standards, procedures, exceptions, implementation plans, and legacy archive records.

04 Take the next action

The user can read, ask a question, request a change, propose a new document, or route a gap to the right owner.

Portal Must Show

Each document detail page should answer the cold-start questions.

Current? Is this the active version?

Show lifecycle state, effective date, publication date, and active source record.

Related? What else should I read?

Show linked policies, standards, procedures, exceptions, and implementation references.

Owner? Who is accountable?

Show document owner, stakeholder groups, governance contact, and review cadence.

Next? What can I do from here?

Offer request, change, exception, or question paths without requiring GitHub training.

What Makes It Work

Findability depends on structured metadata, not just better search UI.

Metadata Why It Matters User Benefit
Domain and topic tags Groups documents by compliance subject area and business language. Users can search the way they think, not by repository structure.
Document type and governance scope Separates policies, standards, procedures, job aids, and non-governed content. Users know whether they are reading an authoritative requirement or supporting guidance.
Related document links Creates explicit relationships between source records instead of relying on memory. Users can move from one document to its dependencies and implementation references.
Owner, stakeholder group, and review date Supports reminders, escalation, ownership checks, and lifecycle monitoring. Users know who maintains the document and whether it is overdue or under review.
Legacy archive and approval evidence Preserves traceability from published output back to history and approval records. Users can trust the portal result and auditors can reconstruct the evidence chain.
